 Faculty bookshelf
  
 
 
 
  [ http://www.amazon.com/The-Spectacle-Late-Maya-Court/dp/0292744366 ]  
    
 
 
 
 Heather Hurst '97  (anthropology) contributed to The Spectacle of the Late Maya Court [ http://utpress.utexas.edu/index.php/books/milspe ] , an oversize book about the ancient Maya murals at Bonampak, which she helped excavate and document, in Mexico. Dating from 800 AD, the paintings show dances, visits of foreign dignitaries, human sacrifice, and other rituals. Along with photos of the now-faded murals, the book features full-color recreations by Hurst and Leonard Ashby, plus extensive artistic, technical, and ethnographic analysis.

 
 
 
    
  [ http://www.amazon.com/Contested-1789-2010-American-Political-University/dp/0700619224 ]  
    
 
 
 
Flagg Taylor (government) is co-author of The Contested Removal Power, 1789–2010 [ http://www.kansaspress.ku.edu/alvcon.html ] , about the history of presidential powers and removal of elected officials. After the impeachment of President Andrew Johnson for dismissing Secretary of War Stanton, the theory of executive power made a resurgence; today, it's still frequently a thorny question. One reviewer called the book "a highly original contribution to presidential studies and American constitutional studies more generally."

 
 
 
  [ http://www.amazon.com/Economic-Growth-Income-Disparity-Bric/dp/981441591X ]  
    
 
 
 
Monica Das (economics) is co-author of Economic Growth and Income Disparity in BRIC: Theory and Empirical Evidence [ http://www.worldscientific.com/worldscibooks/10.1142/8545 ] . The book examines the history of economic fortunes and wealth distribution in Brazil, Russia, India, and China—four of the largest fast-developing economies today. It posits a theoretical framework to help explain when and why a growing national economy plays a role in widening the gap in per-capita incomes.
